## 1. Bohm’s Critique of Fragmentation

David Bohm (1917–1992), one of the most profound theoretical physicists of the 20th century, observed that early civilizations understood reality as a whole. This understanding, he noted, still survives in the Eastern traditions, particularly that of India. But the modern West fragmented this wholeness:

> *“The fragmentation of our world into different nations, ethnic groups, professions, arts, sciences etc., which separate people and things into categories disconnected from each other, has its origin in the kind of thinking which analyses and describes things as being inherently separate and distinct from each other, like parts of a machine.”*

This fragmentary thinking, Bohm argued, is built into the very structure of our language—the subject‑object divide that separates the observer from the observed, the self from the world. And it has led, directly, to the crises we now face:

> *“What is primarily needed is a growing realization of the extremely great danger of going on with a fragmentary process of thought.”*
