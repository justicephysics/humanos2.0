In 2008–09, Mukesh Kumar’s electronics business in Delhi collapsed under the weight of Chinese imports, technology obsolescence, and policy shifts. He returned to his village in Bihar, broke and dislocated, and looked at the education system with fresh eyes.

One day, he formulated the question that changed everything:

> *“Government is elected by people + Government manages education + Education system is ruined = HOW?”*

This was not rhetoric. This was algebra. If A leads to B, and B leads to C, then A should lead to C. But it doesn’t. The equation doesn’t balance.

- **A:** People elect the government (democracy functions).
- **B:** Government manages the education system (constitutional responsibility).
- **C:** Education system is ruined (observable fact).

The voters want good schools. The politicians promise good schools. The government has the constitutional authority and the budget. The buildings exist. The teachers exist. So what is missing?

The recognition was simple and devastating: *“The matter is something else.”* The problem was not a lack of democracy, money, buildings, or teachers. It was something deeper—something everyone was missing.
