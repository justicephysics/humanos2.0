## 1. The Contradiction — Ethics in the Classroom, Extraction in the Colony

The University of Cambridge was founded in 1209. Oxford even earlier. For centuries, these institutions were the intellectual heart of the Western world. They taught theology, philosophy, ethics, law, and political economy. They produced the moral framework that justified governance, shaped policy, and defined what it meant to be “civilized.”

And then the East India Company arrived.

Chartered in 1600, the EIC was a commercial enterprise that became, by 1765, the ruling power of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa. Over two centuries, it drained an estimated $45 trillion from the subcontinent. It engineered famines that killed millions. It systematically dismantled Indian industries so they could not compete with British mills. It extracted timber, cotton, opium, and indigo—and called it “civilization.”

Where were the ethics of Cambridge and Oxford during this? Where was the lecture on the morality of empire? Where was the moral philosophy that condemned the drain of wealth, the destruction of livelihoods, the deliberate starvation of millions?
