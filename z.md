Earth's atmospheric temperature does not decrease uniformly with altitude; instead, it fluctuates between cooling and heating in distinct layers. The temperature drops from $15\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ at the surface, bottoms out at $-90\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ in the mesosphere, and then continuously rises into the thousands of degrees in the uppermost layers. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]  
A stage-by-stage temperature profile of the atmosphere from $0$ to $10,000\text{ km}$ relies on definitions from the International Standard Atmosphere and scientific conventions: [6, 7]  
1. Troposphere ($0\text{ to }11\text{ km}$) 

• Temperature Profile: Decreases from $15\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ (surface) to roughly $-56.5\,^{\circ}\text{C}$. 
• Details: This is the lowest region, heated by Earth's surface. Temperature drops at an average rate of about $6.5\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ per kilometer. Almost all weather occurs here. [8, 9, 10]  

2. Stratosphere ($11\text{ to }50\text{ km}$) 

• Temperature Profile: Starts at $-56.5\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ (at the tropopause), remains steady up to $20\text{ km}$, and then increases to about $-2\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ at $50\text{ km}$. 
• Details: The heating is generated by the ozone layer absorbing ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun. [1, 9, 11, 12, 13]  

3. Mesosphere ($50\text{ to }85\text{ km}$) 

• Temperature Profile: Decreases from $-2\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ to a low of approximately $-90\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $-92\,^{\circ}\text{C}$. 
• Details: This is the coldest layer in the Earth system. Here, air is too thin for ozone heating, and temperatures plummet with altitude. [4, 11, 14, 15, 16]  

4. Thermosphere ($85\text{ to }600\text{ km}$) 

• Temperature Profile: Increases rapidly, starting at $-92\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ and reaching upwards of $1,200\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $2,000\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ (or higher). 
• Details: This dramatic rise is caused by the absorption of intense X-rays and UV radiation directly from the Sun. Despite the extremely high kinetic energy of molecules, the air is so thin that it would not feel "hot" to human touch. [1, 4, 11, 17]  

5. Exosphere ($600\text{ to }10,000\text{ km}$) 

• Temperature Profile: Remains extremely high, often exceeding $2,000\,^{\circ}\text{C}$ at its uppermost boundary. 
• Details: As the outermost layer spanning out toward interplanetary space, molecules are incredibly scarce. The gases, primarily hydrogen and helium, occasionally escape. [1, 18, 19, 20]  

To explore the relationship between the atmosphere and the rest of the planet's systems, consult the Atmosphere of Earth or view an overview of Earth's Atmospheric Layers. [21, 22]
