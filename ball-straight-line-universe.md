## 1. The Ball and the Straight Line — A Simple Analogy

Take an ordinary ball—a sphere of radius 1 meter.

- **Draw a straight line** on its surface, starting at any point, running at exactly **90 degrees** to the underlying Y‑plenum. Keep the line perfectly straight—no bending, no deviation.
- **Let that line extend for a fixed length** of, say, 5 meters.

What happens?

The line will never meet itself. Because it is straight, and because it is drawn at 90 degrees to the plenum, it does not curve back on itself like a great circle on a small sphere. It simply runs straight along the surface. If the surface were infinite, the line would go on forever. But if the total surface area is finite—larger than 5 meters in circumference—the line will eventually reach the end of the available area without ever looping back.

The key insight: **a straight line on a finite, closed surface can be perfectly straight locally, yet still exist within a finite whole.** The straightness is local. The finiteness is global.

## 4. The 90‑Degree Angle — Why the Straight Line Never Meets Itself

You specified that the straight line is drawn at **90 degrees to the Y‑plenum.** This is crucial.

- In a curved space, a line drawn at some other angle might eventually curve back and intersect itself, like a great circle on a globe.
