## 1. The Data — ₹3.5 Lakh Crore, Five Exams, One Extraction Machine

Leader of the Opposition Rahul Gandhi, speaking in Kota, revealed a staggering set of numbers:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Annual spending by aspirants on 5 exams (NEET, JEE, RRB, UPSC, SSC)** | **₹3.5 lakh crore** |
| **Union Government’s entire education budget (2026‑27)** | ~₹1.2 lakh crore (higher education: ₹55,727 cr; IITs: ₹12,123 cr; NITs: ₹6,260 cr) |
| **Ratio** | The coaching industry spends **3×** more than the government spends on education. |

This is not an education system. This is an **examination industry**—a parallel economy built on the desperation of millions. Families borrow, sacrifice, and pour their life savings into preparing children for exams that will reject 97–99.8 % of them. The money flows to coaching centres, hostel owners, publishers, and the tax authorities. The government collects GST on every transaction, income tax on every coaching centre’s profit, and then spends a fraction of that revenue on the very education system that the coaching industry has replaced.

**The coaching industry is funding India’s education system.** The government is not investing in learning; it is collecting revenue from the fear of failure.

## 3. The Revenue Loop — How the Government Profits from Aspirant Desperation

The ₹3.5 lakh crore does not vanish into a black hole. It circulates through a precisely engineered revenue loop:

1. **Aspirants spend** on coaching fees, hostels, books, mock tests, travel.
2. **The coaching industry earns** and pays **income tax**.
3. **Every transaction attracts GST**—the government’s silent share.
4. **The government allocates a fraction** of that revenue to the “education budget.”
5. **The education budget funds the very system** (IITs, NITs, schools) that the coaching industry has rendered irrelevant for actual learning.
6. **The cycle repeats**—with the next batch of aspirants, the next year of desperation, the next ₹3.5 lakh crore.

The government is not a neutral regulator. It is a **beneficiary** of the examination industry. The narrower the gate, the higher the spending, the greater the tax revenue. The system has no incentive to widen the canal—because a wider canal would reduce the revenue. This is the Canal Dogma in its purest fiscal form: **the narrow gate is a profit centre.**
