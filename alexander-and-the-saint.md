## 1. The Ancient Story — Alexander and the Saint

When Alexander the Great invaded India, he had conquered the known world. Kings knelt. Armies scattered. But in India, he heard of a different kind of power—not of swords, but of wisdom. He asked to see the heads of India: the philosophers and saints.

He was taken to the banks of the River Indus, where a naked saint sat. The saint had nothing—not even a loincloth. But his eyes sparkled with a light that seemed to say: *"I bestow grandeur on kings and beauty on the beautiful ones, whenever I look at them."*

Alexander, overwhelmed, offered him kingdoms, wealth, precious stones—anything to accompany him to Greece. The saint smiled. *"I am everywhere. I am omnipresent. I am beyond space."*

Alexander, unaccustomed to refusal, repeated his offer. The saint replied: *"I do not stand in need of anything. I am not a man to like my own discarded sputum."*

Enraged, Alexander drew his sword to kill the saint. The saint burst into laughter. *"You could never have told such a big lie in your life before. I have yet to see a sword which could cut me."*

He explained: *"Children sit on sand, play with it, build houses, and demolish them. The sand does not lose anything. It remains the same. This body is like a house of sand. I am the sand—the base, the all‑pervading. I was never a house. If anyone wants to demolish it, he is damaging his own house. The sand does not run any risk."*

Then the saint said: *"The stars are never separated from the light. So you are myself, and I am yourself."*

Alexander's sword fell from his hand. The conqueror of the world apologised to the naked saint who owned nothing—and everything.
